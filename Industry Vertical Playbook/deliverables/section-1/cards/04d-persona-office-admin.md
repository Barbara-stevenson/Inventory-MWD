# Persona: Office Manager / Administrative Lead
### Card 04d | Section 1: Market & Customer Foundation

**Audience:** Sales reps (secondary contact engagement), marketing (content targeting), onboarding team
**When to use:** Prospect has an office manager or admin who handles day-to-day QB and data entry — often the power user post-sale

**Agent:** Riya — ICP Mapper | Research Squad

---

## Who They Are

**Title variations:** Office Manager, Administrative Manager, Bookkeeper, Office Administrator, Operations Coordinator, AR/AP Manager

**Daily reality:** They're the glue. They enter data into QuickBooks, chase invoices, update spreadsheets, coordinate between departments, and fix things when processes break. They know actual workflows better than the owner because they execute them every day. They'll use Method the most — and resist it hardest if it adds to their workload. Age 30-55, long-tenured, protective of their spreadsheet systems.

---

## What Keeps Them Up at Night

| Pain | Intensity | What they say |
|---|---|---|
| Double data entry between QB and other systems | **Critical** | "I enter every invoice twice." |
| Spreadsheets only they understand | **High** | "Nobody else can use my tracking sheets." |
| Chasing people for information | **High** | "I spend half my day asking salespeople what happened with an order." |
| Errors from manual processes | **High** | "When we miss something, I'm the one who has to fix it." |

---

## What Closes Them / What Loses Them

**Closes:** Seeing QB sync happen in real time. Hearing "you won't enter data twice anymore." Meeting the DEP consultant who'll help them set up. References from other Office Managers who survived the transition.

**Loses:** Being left out of the buying process. Unreliable sync — one glitch during trial and they revert to spreadsheets. Feeling like the system adds work instead of removing it.

---

## Why This Persona Matters for Retention

The Office Manager determines whether a Method account sticks or churns. High adoption = sticky account. Low adoption = parallel spreadsheets = churn risk. Get them comfortable in the first 30 days.

## Appendix: Discovery & Demo Playbook

### Discovery Questions
- "What does your typical day look like — walk me through the data you touch?"
- "How much time do you spend on double entry between QB and other tools?"
- "What's the thing you track in a spreadsheet that you wish was automatic?"
- "What happened the last time you implemented new software?"

### Demo Focus
1. Show QB sync first — two-way, automatic, no double entry
2. Show a workflow they'd recognize: invoice created in Method → appears in QB
3. Show the reports they'd need: AR aging, outstanding invoices, customer activity
4. Emphasize: "Your QB data stays in QB. Method just adds the operational layer."

### What They Want (But May Not Articulate)
- Less data entry — if Method syncs with QB automatically, they get hours back every week
- One place for everything — stop tabbing between five windows
- Reliability — the sync has to work every time, or they'll keep manual backups
- Not to look bad — new software feels like a threat to their competence
- To be included in the decision — they're often left out but will be the primary daily user

### How They Evaluate Software
- Skeptical by default — they've survived bad implementations
- Detail-oriented — they'll test edge cases during trial (unusual invoices, tax exceptions, multi-line orders)
- The first question is always "does it sync with QuickBooks?" The second is "how reliably?"
- They influence the decision — if the Office Manager says "this will make my life harder," the deal is at risk

### Customer Evidence
- **Duraline:** Cross-department adoption (sales, shipping, customer care, AP, AR) — the Office Manager role is central to that high-retention pattern.
