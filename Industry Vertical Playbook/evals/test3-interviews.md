# JTBD Interview Design: Field Services Switching Moment
## For companies (10-50 employees) recently adopting Method

---

## Research Focus & Participant Definition

**Job to study:** The switching moment — understanding what prompted field services companies to move away from their existing way of managing operations (spreadsheets, paper, disconnected tools) and toward Method.

**Participant profile:**
- Company size: 10–50 employees
- Service verticals: HVAC, plumbing, electrical, and similar residential/commercial trade services
- Adoption timeline: Recently adopted Method (within last 6–12 months) — far enough in to have lived with it, recent enough to remember the "before" clearly
- Role: Decision-makers or early power users (owner, operations manager, office manager, or dispatch lead who was part of or influenced the switch)

**Research goal:** Surface the functional, emotional, and social dimensions of why these specific companies made the switch, what held them back initially, and what ultimately tipped the scales.

---

## Interview Questions (Grouped by Theme)

### Context & Triggers (3–4 questions)
These establish how and when the switching need showed up.

1. **"Walk me back to about 6–12 months ago — before you started using Method. What was your operation like day-to-day? How were you managing jobs, scheduling, invoices, that kind of thing?"**

2. **"At what point did you realize your current way of working wasn't cutting it anymore? What happened that made you think, 'We need something better'?"**

3. **"Tell me about a specific moment or situation that made the problem really obvious — maybe a botched job assignment, lost invoice, or frustrated customer. What went wrong, and what was the fallout?"**

4. **"How long had you been dealing with this before you started looking for a solution?"**

### Struggles & Current State (3–4 questions)
These drill into the pains and frustrations that were building pressure.

5. **"What was the biggest headache in your workflow before Method? Was it scheduling, tracking who was where, invoicing, something else?"**

6. **"Tell me about a time when the way you were managing jobs cost you money, time, or a customer. What happened?"**

7. **"How much time were you or your team spending each week on tasks that felt like busywork — data entry, manual scheduling, chasing down paperwork, etc.? What would a better version of that look like?"**

8. **"Were there any jobs or processes that just felt out of your control? Times when you didn't know where a job was, who was handling it, or whether the customer was happy?"**

### Motivations & Desired Outcomes (3–4 questions)
These reveal what "better" meant to them before they landed on Method.

9. **"When you decided to look for a new system, what were you hoping it would solve? What would success look like?"**

10. **"If we could wave a magic wand and fix three things about how you run your business, what would they be?"**

11. **"Think about the ideal day at your business — scheduling runs smoothly, technicians know exactly what to do, invoices go out on time, customers are happy. What would that require on your end? What has to work?"**

12. **"Were there specific outcomes you were tracking or worried about? Like response time, job completion rate, profitability, customer retention?"**

### Alternatives & Workarounds (3–4 questions)
These show what they tried before finding Method, and why it fell short.

13. **"Before Method, what tools or systems did you try to manage your business? Maybe spreadsheets, another software, pen-and-paper, a mix of things?"**

14. **"Why did those tools fall short? What could they do, and what couldn't they do that you needed?"**

15. **"Were there any 'workarounds' you created — things you rigged up or manual processes you built to fill gaps? Tell me about how those worked (and didn't)."**

16. **"Did you look at any other software solutions before landing on Method? What made them not the right fit?"**

### Forces of Progress: Pushes (2–3 questions)
These quantify the pressure to change.

17. **"What was pushing you hardest to make a change? Was it losing customers, wasting time, frustration with your team, something else? Paint a picture of how bad it had gotten."**

18. **"If you had stuck with your old way of working, what would have happened to your business in the next year? Where would you be?"**

### Forces of Progress: Pulls (2–3 questions)
These capture what attracted them to Method specifically.

19. **"What caught your attention about Method? How did you hear about it — did someone recommend it, did you search for alternatives, did a sales rep reach out?"**

20. **"What was the first thing about Method that made you think, 'Yeah, this could work'? Was it a specific feature, a case study, someone's recommendation, a demo?"**

21. **"Walk me through your decision timeline. From the moment you first learned about Method to the moment you decided to implement it — what were the deciding factors? What tipped the scales?"**

### Forces of Progress: Anxieties & Inertia (3–4 questions)
These reveal what almost held them back.

22. **"Before you switched, what worried you about moving to Method? What could go wrong?"**

23. **"Were there people on your team or in your business who were hesitant about making a change? What were they concerned about?"**

24. **"Did you have doubts about whether Method was worth the effort and cost? What might have kept you from going forward?"**

25. **"How much effort did the switch take — setting up, training your team, migrating data, changing habits? Was there a point where you almost walked away?"**

### Implementation & Current State (2–3 questions)
These provide closure and reveal how the job is evolving post-adoption.

26. **"Now that you're using Method, what's different about how you run the business? What's working well?"**

27. **"Are there things you're still figuring out, or ways you're using Method that surprised you compared to what you expected?"**

28. **"Would you recommend Method to other service companies like yours? Why or why not?"**

---

## Interview Logistics

**Duration:** 45–60 minutes per interview  
**Format:** Phone or video call (video preferable for screen sharing if they want to show tools)  
**Recording:** Ask permission to record and transcribe  
**Incentive:** Consider a $25–50 gift card or discount for their time  
**Sample size:** Aim for 8–12 interviews across different service types (2–3 HVAC, 2–3 plumbing, 2–3 electrical, 2–3 mixed/other trades)  

---

## Analysis Approach Post-Interview

Once interviews are conducted, analyze for:

1. **Common triggers** — What situations consistently showed up as the breaking point?
2. **Functional jobs** — What practical progress were they trying to make?
3. **Emotional jobs** — How did they want to feel (control, confidence, less stress)?
4. **Pushing forces** — What specific pains were most urgent?
5. **Pulling forces** — Which Method capabilities or attributes were most compelling?
6. **Adoption friction** — What almost stopped them? What helped them push through?
7. **Patterns by company size or service type** — Do 10-person HVAC shops have different concerns than 40-person electrical firms?

---

## Notes for the Interviewer

- **Listen for stories, not opinions.** "Tell me about a time when…" questions will surface real behavior. Avoid "Do you ever…" or "Would you want…" hypotheticals.
- **Follow the thread.** If they mention a specific failure or frustration, dig in. "What happened next?" and "Why did that matter?" reveal the true job.
- **Watch for contradictions.** If they say "Method is great" but then describe how they're still using spreadsheets for scheduling, that's important. Dig into the gap.
- **Note the tone.** Frustration, relief, pride — emotional language signals where the real pain and gain live.
- **Respect their time.** Have a script and timing, but don't rush through stories. The richness is in the detail.
