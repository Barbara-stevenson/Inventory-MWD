# JTBD Analysis: Precision Metal Works Case Study

## Step 1: Extract Raw Signals

### Situations / Triggers

- Customer calls asking about order status
- Shop floor needs to know what job is next
- New orders received that need estimates
- Work is completed and needs invoicing
- Payments need to be chased

### Struggles, Pains, and Constraints

- Quote: "We were drowning in paper"
- Had to dig through three different spreadsheets to find order status
- Estimating process was "painful" and "took hours"
- Manual re-entry of quote data into QuickBooks caused errors ("wrong quantities, wrong prices")
- Office manager spent 15 hours/week on invoicing alone
- Invoicing delay of 2-3 weeks after shipping parts
- Shop floor had to interrupt owner to ask what to work on next
- Information was scattered across multiple systems
- Risk of data loss in manual translation between systems

### Goals and Desired Outcomes

- Visibility: "pull up their record and see exactly where every order stands"
- Operational clarity: "My shop floor lead uses the Field Crew app on his tablet to see what's next"
- Speed: "Estimates convert straight into work orders and then into invoices — no re-entry"
- Efficiency: "invoicing went from 15 hours a week to maybe 4"
- Cash flow: "we're getting paid faster because invoices go out the same day we ship"
- Peace of mind: "I sleep better. I used to lie awake worrying about what was falling through the cracks"
- Team enablement: "My guys respect the system because it actually makes their jobs easier"

### Alternatives and Workarounds (Current/Old Way)

- Three separate spreadsheets for order tracking
- QuickBooks Desktop for accounting
- Manual data entry and re-entry of quotes
- Cross-referencing work orders with quotes manually
- Manual invoice creation
- Paper-based processes
- Owner as bottleneck for shop floor visibility

### Evaluation of Competing Solutions

- "Looked at Salesforce but it was way too complicated and expensive for us"
- "Tried Jobber but it's really for home services, not manufacturing"
- Method was chosen because of "QuickBooks sync — we didn't want to rip out our accounting system — and the fact that we could customize it to match how we actually work"

## Step 2: Define Core Jobs

### Job 1: See the full picture of ongoing customer orders

**Job story:** "When a customer calls asking about their order status, I want to quickly pull up all the details and know exactly where that order stands, so I can answer the customer immediately and maintain confidence that nothing is being overlooked."

**Functional job:** Access real-time visibility into order status across multiple customers and projects without searching multiple systems.

**Emotional job:** Feel in control and confident that you know what's happening in your business; reduce anxiety about orders slipping through cracks.

**Social job:** Be the kind of business owner/manager who has their operation under control and can give customers immediate, accurate information.

---

### Job 2: Eliminate manual data re-entry and translation errors in the quote-to-invoice workflow

**Job story:** "When we win a new customer quote and need to turn it into a work order and invoice, I want the data to flow automatically through our system without requiring manual re-entry, so I can reduce errors, save time, and get the invoice out quickly."

**Functional job:** Ensure quote data automatically converts into work orders and invoices without manual data entry.

**Emotional job:** Feel confident that the numbers are correct and nothing is getting lost in translation; reduce stress about billing errors.

**Social job:** Be a company that is operationally efficient and doesn't waste customer time or internal resources on redundant work.

---

### Job 3: Reduce administrative overhead on invoicing and payment collection

**Job story:** "When work is completed and shipped, I want the invoicing process to be fast and mostly automated, so I can free up office staff time and get invoices out the same day, which means we get paid faster."

**Functional job:** Complete invoicing and send to customer quickly with minimal manual steps.

**Emotional job:** Feel relief that administrative burden is lighter; take pride in operational efficiency.

**Social job:** Be a business that operates smoothly and respects customer time (invoices come promptly).

---

### Job 4: Give the shop floor clear visibility into what's next without interrupting management

**Job story:** "When a shop floor team member finishes a job, I (as a manager) want them to be able to see the next job on a shared system without having to come ask me, so they can stay productive and I'm not pulled away from other work."

**Functional job:** Make the queue of upcoming work visible on the shop floor in real time.

**Emotional job:** Feel trusted and empowered as a technician; feel respected as a manager whose time is protected.

**Social job:** Be a team that doesn't create unnecessary bottlenecks; be a manager who enables the team rather than gates information.

---

## Step 3: List Desired Outcomes

- Decrease time spent looking up order status by switching between systems
- Increase ability to answer customer inquiries about order status in real time
- Decrease number of data entry errors in the quote-to-work order-to-invoice pipeline
- Decrease time spent on invoicing and billing administration each week
- Increase speed of invoice delivery to customer (reduce days between shipment and invoice)
- Increase speed of customer payment receipt (improve cash flow)
- Reduce anxiety about orders being lost or overlooked
- Make the shop floor job queue visible and accessible without interrupting management
- Increase shop floor team ability to see what's next without asking
- Reduce bottlenecks created by information being held only by the owner
- Increase confidence that the system matches how the business actually works (not forcing a foreign workflow)
- Preserve integration with existing accounting system (QuickBooks) to avoid data silos and rework
- Increase team adoption and respect for the system because it makes their jobs easier, not harder

## Step 4: Map Forces of Progress

### Pushes (away from status quo)

- **Operational chaos:** "Drowning in paper," information scattered across three spreadsheets
- **Customer service friction:** Unable to answer customer questions without research; losing credibility
- **Manual labor tax:** 15 hours/week on invoicing alone, plus time in QuickBooks re-entry and quote building
- **Data loss and errors:** "Half the time something got lost in translation — wrong quantities, wrong prices. We were leaving money on the table."
- **Delayed cash flow:** 2-3 week delay between shipment and invoice = delayed payment
- **Productivity drain:** Shop floor waiting for owner to assign work; owner pulled away from strategic work
- **Owner stress:** Inability to sleep, constant worry about what's being missed
- **Workflow fragmentation:** Quote built in Excel, manually re-entered into QuickBooks, manual invoice creation

### Pulls (toward new solution / toward Method specifically)

- **Demo or peer evidence:** Seeing that a system could give real-time visibility and automate the pipeline
- **Specific promised outcomes:** QuickBooks sync meant no rip-and-replace; customizability meant it could fit their actual workflow, not force a foreign one
- **Speed and ease:** "Estimates convert straight into work orders and then into invoices — no re-entry"
- **Mobile accessibility:** Shop floor crew using tablet app to see next job
- **Immediate business results:** Invoicing time drops from 15 to 4 hours/week; invoices ship same day; payment comes faster
- **Peace of mind:** Owner's stress relief ("I sleep better")
- **Team buy-in:** "My guys respect the system because it actually makes their jobs easier"

### Anxieties (fears about switching)

- **Integration risk:** "We didn't want to rip out our accounting system" — implied concern that a new system would force them to abandon QuickBooks or create double-entry
- **Customization uncertainty:** Concern that out-of-the-box software won't fit their specific manufacturing workflow (evidenced by rejection of Salesforce and Jobber)
- **Team adoption:** Worry that staff would resist a new system that complicates their work
- **Fit for industry:** Jobber "is really for home services, not manufacturing" — concern that generic business tools don't understand manufacturing specifics
- **Cost and complexity:** Salesforce "was way too complicated and expensive" — anxiety about overpaying for unnecessary features

### Habits / Inertia (what kept them doing it the old way)

- **Spreadsheet familiarity:** Excel was something everyone knew how to use (low friction to keep using)
- **QuickBooks sunk cost:** Already invested in QB; familiar with its interface and data structure
- **Ownership bottleneck:** Owner had become the system of record for order status and shop floor direction — changing that requires relinquishing control and trusting the team to use a new tool
- **"It works well enough" mentality:** Despite inefficiencies, the spreadsheet system was functional (just painful and slow)

## Step 5: Surface Opportunity Areas

1. **Help SMB manufacturers reduce administrative overhead in the estimate-to-invoice pipeline by automating data flow between quoting, work orders, and invoicing, eliminating manual re-entry and associated errors.**

2. **Help manufacturing business owners regain confidence and peace of mind by making real-time visibility of all customer orders and project status accessible from a single source, reducing the anxiety of "things falling through the cracks."**

3. **Help manufacturing teams become self-directed and productive by putting the job queue and work-in-progress status on the shop floor (mobile/tablet), eliminating the need to interrupt management for direction.**

4. **Help small manufacturers accelerate cash flow by cutting the time between job shipment and invoice delivery from weeks to the same day, reducing the administrative friction in the invoicing workflow.**

5. **Help manufacturing companies avoid costly system rip-and-replace by offering deep integration with existing accounting systems (QuickBooks) and the ability to customize the workflow to match how the business actually works, not how a generic software vendor thinks it should.**

6. **Help teams adopt new operational systems by designing them to make work easier and more visible, not by adding friction — so that adoption is driven by respect and productivity gain, not mandate.**

## Step 6: App Mapping

### Customers & Leads

**Supports:** Job 1 (visibility into customer/order status)

**Why:** Central repository for customer records and history so anyone in the office can pull up a record quickly instead of searching spreadsheets.

---

### Estimates

**Supports:** Job 2 (eliminate re-entry errors)

**Why:** Allows quotes to be created once and converted into work orders and invoices automatically, eliminating manual re-entry and associated errors.

---

### Work Orders

**Supports:** Job 2 (convert quote to work order) and Job 4 (shop floor visibility)

**Why:** Automatically generated from estimates; can be pushed to Field Crew app on tablets for shop floor visibility.

---

### Invoices

**Supports:** Job 2 (automatic conversion) and Job 3 (reduce admin overhead)

**Why:** Auto-generated from work orders; sent same day of shipment if data flows through the system correctly.

---

### Field Crew (mobile app)

**Supports:** Job 4 (shop floor visibility without management interruption)

**Why:** Puts work queue on the shop floor; shows technicians what's next without needing to ask the owner.

---

### QuickBooks Integration

**Supports:** Anxiety reduction around system switching

**Why:** Syncs data with existing QuickBooks without requiring data migration or dual-entry; eliminates the "rip out accounting system" risk that would have been a deal-breaker.

## Key Insights

**The primary switching moment was not "let's get better software" — it was "we're losing money and sanity to manual process inefficiency."** The pushes (chaos, errors, delay, stress) were strong enough that Mike was willing to evaluate alternatives. But the pulls had to be specific: not "better software" in general, but "software that doesn't force us to replace QuickBooks and actually fits how we work."

**The second critical insight: adoption is not top-down mandate.** Mike says his team "respects the system because it actually makes their jobs easier." This is a powerful social dynamic — the shop floor chose to use Field Crew not because they were forced to, but because it gave them autonomy (they could see what's next without asking the owner).

**The third insight: peace of mind and control are jobs in themselves.** Mike's quote — "The biggest thing honestly is I sleep better. I used to lie awake worrying about what was falling through the cracks" — reveals that the emotional job (reducing anxiety, regaining sense of control) was as important as the functional job (seeing order status). This is often underweighted in product discussions but critical for adoption and loyalty.
